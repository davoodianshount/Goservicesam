Why this matters

Between 1950 and 1980, millions of California homes were built with electrical panels manufactured by Federal Pacific (FPE / Stab-Lok), Zinsco (often labeled GTE-Sylvania), and Challenger. These panels have documented histories of breakers failing to trip during overcurrent conditions — a failure mode that can cause electrical fires inside the wall cavity before anyone notices.

Most California home insurers now decline or non-renew policies on homes with these panels. If you have one, you likely have three reasons to care: fire risk, insurance coverage, and resale value.

What to photograph

  • The outside of the panel door — closed. Shows the overall panel.
  • The inside of the panel door, open — this is where the manufacturer label almost always lives.
  • A straight-on shot of the breakers — with the dead-front cover in place. (Don't remove any cover or panel — that's our job.)
  • Optional: a close-up of any label, stamp, or printing you can see.

Safety note: Do not remove any panel covers. The photos we need are of the panel door (inside & outside) and the face of the breakers with the dead-front cover in place. If the panel is in a garage or closet, make sure you have enough light to get a clear shot.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know if my panel is a Zinsco or Federal Pacific?
The manufacturer name is almost always printed on the inside of the panel door, on a label near the main breaker, or stamped on the metal near the top. Zinsco panels often say "Zinsco" or "GTE-Sylvania" with colorful breaker handles (red, yellow, blue). Federal Pacific panels are labeled "FPE" or "Stab-Lok" and have thin plastic breakers. If you're not sure, send us a photo — we'll tell you within 24 hours.
Is this really free?
Yes. Sending a photo and getting an identification from our licensed electrician costs nothing. If we find that you have a panel we'd recommend replacing, we'll send you a no-obligation quote. You're not on the hook for anything.
Why should I care about an old panel?
Federal Pacific and Zinsco panels were manufactured between roughly 1950 and 1980. Both have documented histories of breakers failing to trip during overcurrent conditions, which can cause electrical fires. Most California home insurers now decline to write or renew policies on homes with these panels. Replacing a known-defective panel reduces fire risk and keeps your insurance policy intact.
